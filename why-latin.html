The question behind the experiment

Why Latin?

Because sometimes the things we recoil from are not dead ends. They are doors we have not yet learnt how to open.

My first reaction to Latin at Mass was resistance rather than wonder. Small amounts of Latin felt unfamiliar, and unfamiliarity can make even a modest change feel larger than it is.

Practice altered that. I learnt the Pater Noster, the Agnus Dei and eventually the Rosary in Latin. Repetition slowly turned strangeness into recognition.

Latin became interesting because repetition turned unfamiliar sounds into recognisable meaning.

What I intend to do

Time permitting, I intend to study three days each week. Some weeks may include more study and some less; this is a real experiment being fitted around ordinary work and life, not a rigid performance schedule.

As I work through Father William Most’s course, I will release completed lessons so that other learners can follow, join in and use the same book alongside the website. Lessons will appear only after I have genuinely studied them and prepared the companion material.

Not a factional project

This is not a campaign about the Traditional Latin Mass or a claim that every Catholic should worship in the same language. Latin remains part of the language, memory and documentary life of the Western Church, and I want to approach it with curiosity rather than discomfort.

What I hope to find

I want to know whether the language can become usable: heard, spoken, prayed and recognised rather than merely decoded. The mistakes, awkward readings and moments of understanding are all part of the evidence.
