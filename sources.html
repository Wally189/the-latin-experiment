Editorial policy

Not every Catholic source says the same kind of thing.

A council document, a canon, a news article and a personal reflection may all be useful. They do not carry the same authority.

Church teaching

When a page explains Catholic doctrine, it should link to the Catechism, Scripture, an ecumenical council, papal teaching or another appropriate Magisterial source. The source must be represented accurately and in context.

Canon and liturgical law

Questions about governance, rights, obligations, sacraments and discipline may require the Code of Canon Law or official liturgical books. Law is not interchangeable with doctrine, and a universal rule may allow exceptions or depend on particular law.

Official news and local Church sources

Vatican News, bishops’ conferences and dioceses show what the living Church is doing and saying. A news story or press release is not automatically an act of the Magisterium. It should be labelled as news or official communication.

History and scholarship

Historical claims should use primary records and responsible scholarship, including non-Catholic work where it is strong. The project should not protect a comforting Catholic story from inconvenient evidence, nor repeat a hostile story without checking it.

Personal reflection

Experience can explain why a subject matters, but it cannot establish doctrine. Personal notes and developing interpretations should be recognisable as such.

The reader should always be able to ask: “Who is claiming this, with what authority, and where can I check it?”
