Editorial policy

Not every Catholic source says the same kind of thing.

A council document, a canon, a news report, a scholarly argument and a personal reflection may all be useful. They do not carry the same authority.

Church teaching

Catechism, councils and appropriate papal teaching.

Church law

Canon Law and official liturgical discipline.

Official communication

Holy See, bishops’ conference and diocesan news.

Scholarship

Historical, philosophical and theological research.

Personal note

The author’s experience, questions and developing work.

Understand

Authority must be named, not implied

When a page explains Catholic doctrine, it should identify the appropriate Magisterial source. When it discusses law, history or news, it should use the source proper to that subject rather than borrowing authority from the word “Catholic”.

Check

Free official source first

Where an official Catholic text is publicly available, the site should link to it directly. A commentary may help the reader, but it should not replace or obscure the original.

Weigh

Catholic sources still require judgement

An official website does not make every sentence Magisterial. A press release is not a dogmatic constitution. A bishop’s prudential judgement is not automatically a universal doctrine. The page should say what kind of source is being used and what it can reasonably establish.

Try

Ask the source question every time

Who is claiming this, with what authority, in what context, and where can I check it?

That question protects both the Church’s teaching and the reader’s freedom.
