My notes

My first encounter with Lesson 1

I was amazed by how much I already understood. English gave me more clues than I expected, as did my limited familiarity with Romance languages. I had imagined the first page of Latin would look entirely foreign, but much of it already felt faintly recognisable.

Where I stumbled was not necessarily the Latin itself. It was the terminology around it. Nominative, case and declension appeared before I really knew what they meant.

I wanted someone to stop and say plainly: this is the doer; this is the action; this is the thing affected. Once I understood that, the technical terms felt much less alarming.

A closer look

The terms I had to stop and check.

What is a case?

A case is a form of a noun that helps show its job in the sentence. English keeps a small trace of this when he becomes him.

What is a declension?

A declension is a family of nouns that changes its endings in a similar pattern. You do not need the full tables yet; simply notice that different nouns can use different endings to do the same job.

Why does Father Most say “objective”?

In this lesson, “objective” refers to the direct object: the person or thing affected by the action. Most modern Latin grammars call this the accusative.

Why do the endings matter?

English relies heavily on word order. Latin can move words around more freely because their forms help identify their roles.

Where are “a”, “an” and “the”?

Latin does not use articles in the same way as English. A natural translation supplies them when the meaning requires one.

Practice

Check the roles.

Mary saw the lamb.

Identify the doer, action and receiver.

Doer: Mary. Action: saw. Receiver: the lamb.

Marcus agnum vīdit.

The sentence means “Marcus saw the lamb.” Which word is doing each job?

Doer: Marcus. Action: vīdit. Receiver: agnum.
