Roman Church Latin in ordinary English

How do I say it?

This guide follows the Italian-influenced pronunciation commonly used for Ecclesiastical Latin. It covers the sounds most likely to appear in Father Most’s course without assuming that the reader knows phonetic symbols.

5

Begin with five steady vowel sounds

Give each vowel one clean sound. Do not let it wander through several sounds as English vowels often do.

The vowels

A, E and I

A like the broad a in “father”. E like “eh”. I like “ee” in “machine”.

agnusAH-nyoos
reginareh-JEE-nah
viditVEE-deet

O, U and Y

O like a clean “oh”. U like “oo” in “rule”. Y is normally treated like Latin i.

DominusDOH-mee-noos
mundusMOON-doos
KyrieKEE-ree-eh

AE and OE

Pronounce these together as a single eh/ay-like sound rather than two separate vowels.

caeliCHAY-lee
poenaPAY-nah

Separate ordinary vowels

When two ordinary vowels meet and are not one of the familiar combinations, let each one be heard.

DeusDEH-oos
Mariamah-REE-ah

The consonants that surprise English speakers

C

Before e, ae, oe, i or y, c sounds like “ch”. Elsewhere it is a hard “k”.

caelumCHAY-loom
caritasKAH-ree-tahs

G

Before e, ae, oe, i or y, g sounds like the j in “gentle”. Elsewhere use a hard g.

reginareh-JEE-nah
gloriaGLOH-ree-ah

GN and SC

GN sounds like the middle of “canyon”. SC before e or i commonly sounds like “sh”.

agnusAH-nyoos
descenditdeh-SHEN-deet

TI before another vowel

In many words, ti followed by another vowel becomes “tsee”. Keep ordinary “tee” after s, t or x.

gratiaGRAH-tsee-ah
hostiaOHS-tee-ah

J and QU

J sounds like English y. QU is “kw”.

JesusYEH-soos
quikwee

PH, TH and CH

PH is f. TH is an ordinary t. In Greek-derived words, CH is normally a hard k.

ChristusKREES-toos

Stress without panic

Two syllables

Stress the first syllable: AG-nus, CHRIS-tus.

Longer words

The original scanned edition often marks the stressed syllable. Use that mark. Without it, stress depends on the second-to-last syllable and becomes easier through repeated examples.

A practical standard, not a reason to freeze: Latin has been pronounced differently across countries and centuries. This page uses the Roman style common in modern Catholic worship. Clear vowels and consistency matter more than an exaggerated accent.

A 30-second practice routine

  1. Say the word slowly and give every vowel a clean sound.
  2. Check whether c, g, sc, gn or ti changes the sound.
  3. Say the whole word again naturally.
  4. Record one attempt before study and another afterwards. Compare clarity, not theatricality.
