Roman Church Latin in ordinary English

How do I say it?

This guide follows the Italian-influenced pronunciation commonly used for Ecclesiastical Latin. It covers the sounds most likely to appear in Father Most’s course without assuming that the reader knows phonetic symbols.

5

Begin with five steady vowel sounds

The safest habit is to give each vowel one clean sound. Do not let it wander through several sounds as English vowels often do.

The vowels

A, E and I

A like the broad a in “father”. E like “eh”, without turning it into “ee”. I like “ee” in “machine”.

agnusAHG-noos
reginareh-JEE-nah
viditVEE-deet

O, U and Y

O like a clean “oh”. U like “oo” in “rule”. Y, when it appears, is normally treated like Latin i.

DominusDOH-mee-noos
mundusMOON-doos
KyrieKEE-ree-eh

AE and OE

These combinations are normally pronounced together as a single eh/ay-like sound rather than as two separate English vowels.

caeliCHAY-lee
poenaPAY-nah

Two vowels can remain separate

When two ordinary vowels meet and are not one of the familiar combinations, let each one be heard. Move cleanly from one to the next.

DeusDEH-oos
Mariamah-REE-ah

The consonants that surprise English speakers

C changes before certain vowels

Before e, ae, oe, i or y, pronounce c like “ch”. Elsewhere it is a hard “k”.

ecclesiaehk-KLAY-zee-ah
caelumCHAY-loom
caritasKAH-ree-tahs

G follows a similar pattern

Before e, ae, oe, i or y, pronounce g like the “j” in “gentle”. Elsewhere use a hard “g”.

reginareh-JEE-nah
gloriaGLOH-ree-ah

GN sounds like “ny”

Treat gn rather like the middle of “canyon”. Do not give the letters two heavy English sounds.

agnusAH-nyoos
magnusMAH-nyoos

SC can become “sh”

Before e or i, sc is commonly pronounced “sh”. Elsewhere keep the harder “sk” sound.

descenditdeh-SHEN-deet
scutumSKOO-toom

TI before another vowel

In many words, ti followed by another vowel becomes “tsee”. Keep an ordinary “tee” sound after s, t or x.

gratiaGRAH-tsee-ah
hostiaOHS-tee-ah

J, QU, PH, TH and CH

J sounds like English “y”. QU is “kw”. PH is “f”. TH is an ordinary “t”. In Greek-derived words, CH is normally a hard “k”.

JesusYEH-soos
quikwee
ChristusKREES-toos

Stress without panic

Two syllables

Stress the first syllable: AG-nus, CHRIS-tus. You do not need to calculate anything.

Longer words

The original scanned edition often marks the stressed syllable. Use that mark. Without it, Latin stress depends on the second-to-last syllable; this becomes easier through repeated examples rather than memorising a formula on day one.

A practical standard, not a reason to freeze: Latin has been pronounced differently across countries and centuries. This guide uses the Roman style common in modern Catholic worship. Clear, steady vowels and consistent practice matter more than performing an exaggerated Italian accent.

A 30-second practice routine

  1. Say the word slowly, giving every vowel its own clean sound.
  2. Notice whether c, g, sc, gn or ti changes the sound.
  3. Say the whole word again without stretching the stressed syllable unnaturally.
  4. Record one attempt before study and another afterwards. Compare clarity, not theatricality.
