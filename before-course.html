The absolute basics

Five ideas, in ordinary English.

1. A noun

A person, place, thing or idea: Mary, Rome, rose, faith.

2. A verb

The action or state: carries, sees, is, prayed.

3. A case

The form a noun takes to show the job it is doing. English has traces of this: he becomes him.

4. A declension

A family of nouns that changes endings in a similar pattern. You do not need to memorise all the families today.

5. An ending

Latin often places grammatical clues at the end of a word. The ending is not decoration; it helps reveal the word’s job.

6. A macron

A line over a vowel—such as ā—showing vowel length. TLE may use it as a learning aid, while preserving quoted texts as they appear in the source.

The sentence shape

Doer → action → receiver

This is not the only kind of sentence, nor must Latin always appear in this order. It is simply the clearest shape for noticing what each word is doing.

Maria carried a rose.
Maria is the doer. Carried is the action. A rose receives the action.
01

The doer

The doer is called the nominative.

Ask: who or what performed the action?

02

The action

The action is expressed by the verb.

Father Most begins with actions that have already happened.

03

The receiver

The receiver is called the object—or accusative.

Father Most may call this the “objective” case. Modern grammars usually say “accusative”.

Pronunciation

Enough to begin aloud

TLE will eventually include Alan’s checked learner recordings. For now, read slowly and remember that Ecclesiastical Latin usually follows an Italian-influenced sound system.

C
c before e, i, ae or oe

Usually sounds like “ch”: caelum.

G
g before e or i

Usually sounds soft, as in “gem”.

V
v sounds like English v

This is the normal Ecclesiastical convention used by TLE.

Do not wait for perfect pronunciation.

Read aloud carefully, listen, correct yourself and keep going. The experiment should show improvement rather than pretend that a beginner begins as an authority.
