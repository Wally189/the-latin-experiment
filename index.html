A beginner's Catholic learning journey

Can I learn the language of the Church?

I am not a Latin scholar, a teacher or a liturgical campaigner. I am an ordinary British-Irish Catholic with a passion for the Church, wondering whether a supposedly “dead” language can become alive enough for me to understand, speak and pray.

Why this experiment?

Latin is not merely an aesthetic.

I encounter it in the Mass, sacred music, prayers, inscriptions, Church documents and the history of Western Christianity. I want to understand more than isolated phrases—and to see whether a busy adult can learn through steady, ordinary work.

This is deliberately not a traditionalist project. The Church has prayed in many languages from the beginning. My interest is simpler: Latin is part of our inheritance, and I would like to know it rather than admire it from a distance.

The wider Catholic context

The Church has called herself a custodian and promoter of Latin.

TLE treats that inheritance as something to explore intelligently and generously—not as a weapon in arguments about identity or liturgy.

The Julie & Julia idea

Father Most wrote the course. I am attempting the journey.

The source remains the source

Father William Most’s course keeps its name, sequence and authorship. TLE explains why I chose it, links to lawful ways of finding it, and never quietly republishes it as my own.

The experiment supplies the human trail

I record what I understood, what frightened me, the terminology I had to look up and the connections I began to notice. The companion is built around real beginner needs rather than an imaginary perfect student.
