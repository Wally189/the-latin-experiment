TLE explanation

Build the sentence by asking two ordinary questions.

Latin endings carry information that English often communicates through word order. Begin with meaning, then notice the endings.

  • Who did it? Look for the nominative.
  • What received the action? Look for the accusative.

A closer look

Open only what helps.

The main route is enough for a first pass. These notes are here when a question is getting in the way.

Why begin with a completed action?+

A completed action gives a beginner a concrete mini-story: somebody acted and something was affected. The verb ending can be recognised as a single unit before the full verb system is introduced.

Do Latin sentences always follow this word order?+

No. Latin word order is flexible because endings identify grammatical roles. TLE examples may use a clear beginner-friendly order, but the endings—not position alone—tell you what each noun is doing.

What do “first, second and third declension” mean?+

They are families of noun forms. Nouns in each family change their endings in recognisable patterns. Lesson 1 lets you meet singular forms from three families in actual sentences before asking you to master full tables.

TLE practice

Try first. Reveal second.

These are original TLE examples, not sentences reproduced from Father Most.

01

Petrus librum portāvit.

Identify the doer and the receiver.

Doer: Petrus. Receiver: librum. The completed action is portāvit.
02

Rēx lēgem servāvit.

Which word is nominative, and which is accusative?

Nominative: rēx. Accusative: lēgem. Both are third-declension forms.
03

Translate the shape, not just the words.

“The girl praised the poet.” Who did what to whom?

The girl is the doer; the poet receives the action; “praised” is a completed action. A later step is choosing the correct Latin forms.
