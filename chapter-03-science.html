Before Catholicism

Is faith opposed to science?

The familiar story is a long war between evidence and religion. The real history contains conflict, cooperation, error and questions belonging to different kinds of inquiry.

Look

Separate the scientific method from a philosophy of reality

Science investigates the natural world through observation, measurement, modelling and test. It does not need a theological method to date a rock, describe a virus or examine a star.

Questions about why there is an intelligible natural order, what counts as explanation, and what human beings ought to do with technology are not answered by measurement alone.

Ask

Are the claims competing—or answering different parts of the same reality?

Religious claims can collide with scientific findings when Scripture or tradition is interpreted badly. Scientific findings can also be stretched into philosophical conclusions that the evidence itself does not establish.

Understand

Catholic teaching expects genuine truth to be coherent

The Church teaches that faith and reason cannot finally contradict one another because truth cannot contradict truth. Apparent conflict may require correction of a scientific claim, a theological interpretation or the reasoning joining them.

This is not a guarantee that Catholic institutions have always behaved intelligently. It is the standard against which their behaviour can be judged.

Weigh

History is more useful when it remains complicated

The Galileo affair involved serious institutional failure and should not be reduced to a harmless misunderstanding. It also does not describe every relationship between Catholicism and science.

“The Church invented science” and “the Church always opposed science” are both slogans. A useful account has to examine particular people, institutions, texts and decisions.

Try

Separate a finding from the meaning attached to it

  1. Choose one subject often presented as a religion-versus-science conflict.
  2. Write the scientific finding in one sentence.
  3. Write the philosophical or theological conclusion commonly drawn from it.
  4. Ask whether the finding alone establishes that conclusion.

Review

A Catholic need not fear a fact. The harder task is to understand what kind of question the fact answers, what it means, and what should be done with it.
