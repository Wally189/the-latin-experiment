Before Catholicism

Is faith opposed to science?

The familiar story is a long war between evidence and religion. The real history contains conflict, cooperation, error, discovery and questions that belong to different kinds of inquiry.

Science studies the natural world

Science asks how observable reality behaves. It tests explanations against evidence, revises models and produces knowledge with extraordinary practical power. Catholic belief does not provide an alternative method for measuring a virus, dating a rock or describing a star.

Theology and philosophy ask further questions: why there is an intelligible natural order, what counts as an explanation, what human knowledge is for, and whether moral limits apply to what technology makes possible.

Different questions can still collide

It is too easy to say that science and religion occupy completely separate compartments. Religious people make claims about creation and human beings; scientific findings can expose bad interpretations. Scientific claims can also be stretched into philosophical conclusions that the evidence alone does not establish.

The Catholic position is that genuine truth cannot contradict genuine truth. When conflict appears, an interpretation of Scripture, a scientific claim, or the reasoning connecting them may require correction.

The history is not a slogan

The Galileo affair involved real institutional failure and should not be turned into a harmless misunderstanding. It also does not describe every relationship between Catholicism and scientific study. Catholic institutions, clergy and laypeople have contributed to universities, astronomy, genetics, medicine and other fields, while at other times resisting evidence or behaving defensively.

History becomes useful when it is allowed to remain complicated. Neither “the Church invented science” nor “the Church always opposed science” is an adequate account.

What remains outside a scientific measurement

Science can describe the neural activity associated with love, but not decide whether a person ought to be loved. It can model the effects of a weapon, but not determine whether using it is just. It can investigate the beginning of the observable universe, but the philosophical question of why anything exists remains.

This is not a licence to insert God wherever knowledge is incomplete. It is a recognition that not every meaningful question is a scientific question.

A Catholic need not fear a fact. The harder work is deciding what the fact means and what should be done with it.
