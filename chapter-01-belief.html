Before Catholicism

What do we mean by belief?

Religious arguments often begin too late. Before asking whether Catholicism is true, it helps to notice how belief already works in ordinary life.

Look

Belief already surrounds ordinary life

We rely on memory, testimony, expertise, inference and trust every day. Very little of what any person knows has been checked personally from first principles.

That does not make every belief equal. It means evidence normally reaches us through judgement rather than bypassing it.

Ask

What separates belief from guesswork?

Is a belief reasonable because it feels certain, because most people share it, because an expert says so, or because the available reasons are strong?

The useful question is not who has beliefs. Everyone does. The useful question is which beliefs are true and what supports them.

Understand

Catholic faith is not described as a blind impulse

Catholic teaching presents faith as a free human response to God involving intellect and will, made possible by grace. It does not ask a person to call a contradiction true or to pretend that evidence does not matter.

This does not yet prove that God has revealed anything. It states the kind of claim Catholicism asks us to examine.

Weigh

Inherited religion and inherited secularism can both avoid scrutiny

Religion may be accepted through fear, family loyalty or habit. A secular outlook may also carry unexamined assumptions about what counts as evidence, whether only measurable things are real, and whether moral truth is more than preference.

A fair beginning makes both sets of assumptions available for examination.

Try

Audit one belief

  1. Choose one important belief you hold about the world.
  2. List the evidence, testimony and assumptions supporting it.
  3. Now choose one belief you associate with Catholicism and identify where you first acquired it.
  4. Mark what you know, what you infer and what you have never checked.

Review

Belief is not the absence of evidence. Human knowledge normally combines evidence, trust and judgement. Catholic faith makes a particular claim about a free, reasonable response to God—and that claim can now be examined.
