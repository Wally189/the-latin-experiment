Reading · Augustine’s Confessions

Why write a confession addressed to God?

My first question was whether Augustine’s book was a private sacramental confession made public. It is not. The work is prayer addressed to God, but composed for human readers. Augustine recounts sin, memory, conversion and grace so that praise and confession become inseparable.

The strange effect is that the reader overhears a life being interpreted before God. That may explain why the prose feels both intensely personal and deliberately rhetorical: Augustine is not merely listing events; he is asking what a human life means when remembered in the presence of God.

To verify: distinguish the literary and theological senses of confessio, and compare this reading with reliable Augustinian scholarship.

Memory · Mass

What first felt foreign, and what later became home

For a newcomer, the Mass can appear to assume knowledge: gestures, responses, silences and movements already understood by everyone else. The temptation is to judge the experience solely by whether it feels immediately welcoming or comprehensible.

Repeated attendance changes the perception. What seemed like exclusion can become a stable form into which the individual is received. The task is to explain the form without pretending that mystery can be reduced to instructions.

To develop: a first-person account of attending Mass before reception, with a companion page explaining the structure of the liturgy.

Project note

Evangelisation without salesmanship

The site does not need to speak as though every visitor is a prospect being moved through a conversion funnel. The more credible witness may be to show Catholicism being taken seriously: its claims examined, its sources read, its prayers learned and its difficulties admitted.

The personal story is the doorway. Evangelisation, where it occurs, is the consequence of making the faith visible as coherent, demanding and alive.
